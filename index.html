Training philosophy
🔁
Consistency over volumeThree focused running sessions per week beats six mediocre ones. Every session has purpose — none are junk miles.
🏑
Hockey is a training assetTuesday/Thursday hockey delivers interval-quality cardio and agility. Matches add aerobic load. Treat them as part of the program, not obstacles to it.
📈
Aerobic foundation firstThe marathon is won at easy pace. Long runs build the fat-burning engine and teach the body to run on tired legs — the defining skill of marathon racing.
🛡️
Durability over heroicsArriving at the start line healthy beats one great workout followed by injury. Cutback weeks and easy days are not optional.
🌙
Recovery is trainingMonday is sacred rest. Sleep, nutrition, and the Friday recovery jog matter as much as Wednesday's quality session. Adaptation happens during recovery.
🎯
Race-specific preparationFrom week 12, marathon pace work is woven into long runs so race-day effort feels familiar. By taper, Tegan knows exactly what 5:05/km feels like over 42 km.

Training pace guide

Based on a 3:37 target (midpoint of goal range) and the benchmark of 14 km at 5:00/km at controlled HR ~170. Use HR as the governor — not ego. On cold mornings or after tough hockey matches, run by feel.

Easy / recovery
5:45–6:15 /km
Fully conversational. HR 130–145. Used on Friday recovery runs and the opening 60–70% of long runs. The most important pace in the program.
Long run (aerobic)
5:30–6:00 /km
Easy but purposeful. HR 140–152. The bread and butter of marathon prep. Resist every urge to go faster.
Marathon pace (MP)
5:00–5:19 /km
Comfortably hard. Full sentences possible. HR 155–165. Introduced in long runs from week 12. By race day this should feel like a controlled cruise.
Tempo / threshold
4:30–4:45 /km
Comfortably uncomfortable. Short sentences only. HR 165–173. Wednesday quality sessions. Raises lactate threshold — the key to holding MP for 42 km.
Intervals (10 km effort)
4:15–4:30 /km
Hard. One-word answers. HR 173–182. Short reps only. Used sparingly — hockey provides interval quality already.
Hockey training equiv.
Tempo–interval
Sprints, direction changes, and drills count as quality. Don't stack hard Wednesday sessions in weeks where hockey intensity is very high.
Training phases
Phase 1 — Foundation
Pre-Marathon Foundation
18 May – 21 June · Weeks 1–5
Build consistent running habits around hockey. No pace pressure — the goal is the weekly routine, time on feet, and establishing strength work. Long runs grow 12→18 km. Week 3 is a hockey bye — a gift. Week 4 is a cutback week.
  • Run 3× per week consistently
  • Long run reaches 18 km by week 5
  • Establish strength training routine
  • First fueling practice (from 14 km+)
  • Weekly running volume: 25–34 km
Phase 2 — Build
Marathon Block: Build
22 June – 9 August · Weeks 6–12
Structured marathon training begins. Tempo running introduced. Long runs build to 26 km. Week 7 is another bye. Week 8 has the only Sunday game — long run shifts to Saturday. Two cutback weeks manage cumulative fatigue.
  • Introduce continuous tempo (6–8 km)
  • Long run builds to 26 km
  • Cutback weeks at weeks 8 and 11
  • Gel practice every long run from 18 km+
  • Weekly running volume: 31–46 km
Phase 3 — Peak
Marathon Block: Peak
10 August – 20 September · Weeks 13–18
Most demanding phase. All remaining regular season games are Saturdays — long runs stay Sunday. Hockey finals (~29 Aug, 5 Sep, 12 Sep) require managed running volume. After grand final, hockey ceases — Saturday opens up for running.
  • MP segments in long runs (weeks 13+)
  • Peak long runs: 28 km (Wk 12), 30 km (Wk 13), 32 km (Wk 14), 34 km (Wk 16)
  • Reduce running during finals (weeks 15–17)
  • Post-hockey: add Saturday running (week 18)
  • Weekly running volume: 34–58 km
Phase 4 — Taper
Taper & Race
21 September – 11 October · Weeks 19–21
3-week taper. Hockey season is over. Volume drops sharply but intensity maintained with short race-pace efforts. The body consolidates all adaptations. Taper madness is normal — trust the training. Race day arrives.
  • Volume cuts: –30% then –50% from 34 km peak
  • Short MP sharpeners keep legs race-ready
  • Dial in race-day nutrition, kit, logistics
  • Rest, sleep, hydrate, carb-load final 2 days
  • Race week: ~12 km + marathon
🏒 Hockey finals — weeks 15–17 (est. 29 Aug, 5 Sep, 12 Sep)

Finals venues and times are TBC but expected to be Saturdays. During finals weeks, Wednesday running is dialled back and long runs are capped at 22–26 km. The final itself is a high-quality training stimulus — treat it that way. After the grand final (~12 Sep), hockey ceases. Tuesdays and Thursdays free up and Saturday becomes a running day — a genuine performance opportunity for the final build before taper.

Friday strength session (45–55 min)

Performed before the easy 5–6 km recovery run. Three pillars: single-leg stability, posterior chain strength, and calf resilience — all critical for a hybrid hockey/marathon athlete.

🦵 Single-leg strength
  • Bulgarian split squat — 3 × 8–10/side
  • Single-leg Romanian deadlift — 3 × 8/side
  • Step-up with knee drive — 3 × 10/side
  • Pistol squat progression (assisted → free)
🔗 Posterior chain
  • Hip thrust / barbell glute bridge — 3 × 12
  • Nordic hamstring curl — 3 × 5–8
  • Deadlift (moderate load) — 3 × 6
  • Cable pull-through — 3 × 12
🦶 Calf resilience
  • Single-leg eccentric calf raise — 3 × 15/side
  • Tibialis anterior raise — 3 × 15
  • Pogo hops → single-leg hops — 3 × 20
  • Calf raise on decline board — 3 × 12
🏋️ Core stability
  • Dead bug — 3 × 10/side
  • Copenhagen plank — 3 × 20–30 s/side
  • Single-leg glute bridge — 3 × 12/side
  • Pallof press — 3 × 10/side
📈 Progression by phase
  • Weeks 1–5: Learn movements, light load, form
  • Weeks 6–12: Add load progressively
  • Weeks 13–17: Maintain — don't peak strength
  • Weeks 18–21: 2–3 key exercises only
⚡ Hockey synergy
  • Nordic curls: protect hamstrings from sprint loads
  • Copenhagen planks: protect adductors (hockey-specific)
  • Calf eccentrics: prevent Achilles / shin issues
  • Glute strength: improves economy + lateral cutting
💧 Fueling strategy
During training
  • Under 60 min — water only
  • 60–90 min — 1 gel at 45 min, water throughout
  • 90+ min — gel every 30–40 min from 45 min mark
  • Aim for 40–60 g carbohydrate per hour on long runs
  • Always use the exact gel you'll race with
  • Never try new nutrition on race day
Race day plan
  • Pre-race: familiar carb-rich meal 2–3 hrs before
  • Gel at km 5, then every 6 km (~every 30 min)
  • Sip at every aid station — do not skip any
  • Electrolytes if warm (salt tabs or sports drink)
  • Melbourne October avg: 13–18°C — manageable
  • Practice drinking while running from week 14+
🛌 Recovery protocols
Weekly habits
  • Monday: full rest, 10–15 min mobility
  • Post-hockey: protein + carbs within 30 min
  • Post-long run: legs elevated, protein, carbs, sleep
  • 7–9 hrs sleep per night — this is training
  • 5 min daily foam roll: calves, hamstrings, glutes
Warning signs — reduce load
  • Resting HR elevated more than 5 bpm above normal
  • Persistent heavy legs beyond 48 hours
  • Disrupted sleep despite physical tiredness
  • Loss of motivation — dreading sessions
  • Any sharp or localised pain: rest and assess
🏁 Race strategy — executing 3:30–3:45
Pacing plan
  • Target even splits or a very slight negative split
  • km 1–5: run by feel — crowds pull you too fast
  • km 5–30: lock in 5:00–5:19/km regardless of how good you feel
  • km 30–35: form, fuel, one kilometre at a time
  • km 35–42: run by feel — trust the training
  • If you feel great at km 30, you can push — not before
Mental strategy
  • Break the race into thirds: easy, steady, earn it
  • The "wall" is a fueling and pacing problem — solve it in training
  • Prepare a power phrase for km 32–37
  • Focus only on what you control: pace, form, fueling
  • Hockey taught you to compete under pressure — use that
  • Taper doubts and phantom niggles are normal
👟 Gear & logistics
Shoes
  • Begin long runs in race shoes from week 12
  • Never debut race shoes on race day
  • At least 80–100 km on them by 11 October
  • Wear race-day socks in training too
Race day checklist
  • Gels pre-loaded into pockets or race belt
  • Vaseline / Body Glide: inner thighs, underarms, nipples
  • Throwaway layer if cold at start (discard km 1–2)
  • Arrive 90 min early — Melbourne CBD start is busy
